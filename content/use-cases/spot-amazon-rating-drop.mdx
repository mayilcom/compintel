## The problem

A competitor's hero SKU is sitting at 4.4 stars. Over 4 weeks, 47 new 1-star reviews pile up citing broken packaging on Blinkit. The rating drops to 4.1. Their product page sees a 22% drop in conversion.

Those dissatisfied customers are in the market for an alternative right now.

Most brand teams find out about this three weeks later — when someone on the team happens to check the competitor's listing.

## Why this is time-sensitive

Amazon's algorithm responds quickly to rating drops. When a product drops below 4.2, it loses prominent placement in category search results. When it drops below 4.0, it effectively stops appearing in top-of-search for category terms.

This creates a window — sometimes 2–4 weeks — where the competitor's organic visibility drops, their conversion falls, and customers are actively looking for alternatives. That window closes when they fix the issue and reviews recover.

## What to watch

**Rating delta** — The week-over-week change in average rating. A drop of 0.2+ on a hero SKU in a single week is a significant signal.

**Review velocity** — How many new reviews this week vs. the 4-week average. A spike in review velocity with negative sentiment means a specific issue triggered a wave of responses.

**Negative review themes** — What customers are complaining about: packaging, taste, delivery, wrong product. Packaging complaints on quick commerce are particularly actionable — the competitor has a fulfilment problem you can exploit.

## The response

When this signal appears:

1. **Check your own listing** — Are you appearing above them in category search now?
2. **Run a quick commerce promotion** — A targeted discount or bundle on Blinkit or Swiggy Instamart captures switchers immediately
3. **Flag to your agency** — A retargeting campaign targeting competitor product page visitors (via custom audiences) can intercept defecting customers
4. **Monitor for recovery** — If their reviews recover in 2–3 weeks, the window is closing
